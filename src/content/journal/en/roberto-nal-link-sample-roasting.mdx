<DropCap>Sample roasting is what separates a serious roastery from a producer of toasted coffee.</DropCap>

Before a lot ever reaches production, before a coffee appears on a bar's menu, before the team even decides whether it's worth buying, someone roasts it small. Two hundred grams. Sometimes less. They smell it at the end of first crack, taste it the next day, adjust one variable, and start again. That's the daily routine of a roaster who takes the craft seriously.

Roberto Nal works at **Tres Fases Tostadores** and builds profiles there, batch by batch, before a single kilo crosses to the production roaster. We asked him to walk us through what he looks for in every sample, and why sample roasting is what decides which coffees make it to the menu.

## Why sample roasting matters

A production roaster is built to repeat. Once the profile is locked (ramp, decline, development, drop) the machine delivers it batch after batch. But the profile doesn't appear out of nowhere. It has to be discovered first, and discovering a profile on twelve kilos of coffee is expensive, slow, and imprecise. A sample roaster compresses that search: you can roast the same origin three, four, five times in a morning, changing only one variable at a time. What would take a week in production takes a session on a sample roaster.

There's another, less visible reason. Sample roasting is the quality-control tool *before* a purchase decision. When a supplier sends samples of a new origin, the first step is to roast them all on the same neutral profile, identically, and cup them on the table. Without a tuned sample roaster, that decision turns into noise. It ends up depending on how the curve happened to land that morning, not on the coffee itself.

## The Link in practice

**The Link** is built for that routine. It's a small single-drum sample roaster sized for evaluation batches: large enough that the heat behaves the way it will in production, small enough that running three back-to-back batches in an hour is realistic. The curve reads cleanly, the controls respond fast, and the between-batch cleanup doesn't break the rhythm.

It doesn't replace a production roaster. It isn't meant to. What it offers is the workbench where the profile is rehearsed. The decision about **what** to produce happens there, before the decision about **how** to produce it ever reaches the big machine.

<Callout variant="tip" title="For small roasteries">
  A sample roaster doesn't replace your production line. It's the tool that validates what's worth producing. Without one, every purchase decision and every new profile gets paid for in production-batch mistakes.
</Callout>

## What ends up in the cup

Sample roasting is repetition until the coffee says what it has to say. It's patience disguised as a technical routine. The difference between a roastery that grows and one that plateaus almost always shows up here: not in the biggest machine in the room, but in the small one in the corner where someone spends the morning tasting, taking notes, and starting over.

That's what Roberto does. That's what the Link, used well, makes possible every day.
