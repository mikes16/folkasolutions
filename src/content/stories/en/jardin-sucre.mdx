<DropCap>Jardín Sucre started from a simple question: why was there almost no French pastry in Monterrey?</DropCap>

Samim is 28, French, and moved to the northeast of Mexico. He spent some time working in Luxembourg, returned to Monterrey, and along with Dominique and Mauricio began shaping the project. The idea was not to open another café with croissants on the side. It was to bring an entire culture in, without stepping on the one already there.

## A French pâtisserie with a Mexican address

The shop sits at Río Guadalquivir 136, in Del Valle. From outside it could look like one more bakery, but the conversation it has with the neighborhood is very specific. For Samim, the move back to Mexico was not just a professional decision. It was returning to a place he already loved, with a culture he recognized and respected.

<PullQuote attribution="Samim · Jardín Sucre">
  I loved the culture, I loved the people. Here is a bit like in France: it is a culture that matters.
</PullQuote>

That relationship shapes how they bake. The technique is French: lamination, butter, the precise geometry of a properly made croissant. The conversation, on the other hand, runs in both directions.

## The croissant de muertos as a gesture

The example they bring up themselves is the croissant de muertos. A piece built on French technique, set inside a Mexican date. It is not a decorative wink. It is the way they understand what they are doing: bringing their bread, their pastry, their way of working dough, and doing it inside a calendar that is not their own.

<PullQuote attribution="Samim · Jardín Sucre">
  What we are trying to do is bring our French culture while sharing the Mexican one.
</PullQuote>

He says it without ceremony, but the line explains almost the whole menu. The new products they create are designed, in his words, "so as not to appropriate your culture, but on the contrary, to share it." It is a small distinction in language and a very large one in practice.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Why context matters">
  A bakery that travels without context stays exotic. One that lands on its block, reads the date, reads the street, and lets the recipe move a little to fit, stays.
</Callout>

## Two years, three people, one table

More than two years of work by the time Samim films the video. Him, Dominique, and Mauricio. A small French pâtisserie in San Pedro Garza García, set on avoiding both the tourist postcard and the empty fusion. The premise that lands is the one that matters: the bread they serve speaks French, but the table is set right here.
